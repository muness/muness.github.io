I have a recurring experience in technical and product conversations:
I propose something I’m proud of, and I get blank stares back.

Not pushback. Not debate. Just… nothing.

For a long time I interpreted that as a personal failure: I must be explaining it badly. I must not be credible. I must be missing something obvious.

But a friend gave me a framing that finally clicked:

> You’re selling electricity to people who haven’t seen a lightbulb yet.

That single line explains a lot of “invisible” moments.

It also explains a bunch of frustrating “AI adoption” moments.
If you’re trying to get people to *harness* agentic tools, and they respond with “meh”, it’s tempting to think they’re uninterested.
Often they just can’t *see* the move yet.

## Problem Space vs Solution Space

When I say “comprehension gap”, I don’t mean intelligence. I mean *frames*.

- **Problem space:** what constraints are real, what outcomes matter, what tradeoffs exist.
- **Solution space:** what mechanisms are available, what architectures are plausible, what moves exist.

If what you say lands far outside the listener’s current solution space, they can’t evaluate it. Sometimes they can’t even *parse* it. So it doesn’t register as “wrong.” It registers as “noise.”

Persuasion is not mainly “having better arguments.”
It’s *bringing someone across a representational gap*.

## This Isn’t About Faster Agents

When people talk about AI tooling, the energy often goes toward speed: “how do we make the agent move faster?”

But generation is already cheap. Drafts are abundant. The binding constraint is not agent speed.

The binding constraint is *our ability to steer, verify, and stabilize* what agents produce:

- Can we state intent clearly enough that the work converges?
- Can we spot drift early enough to abort without losing learning?
- Can we install guardrails and defaults so the next person doesn’t have to reinvent the loop?

That is a human capability problem, not a model latency problem.
And capability is gated by comprehension: the move has to become thinkable before it becomes habitual.

## A Ladder, Not a Leap

If the gap is comprehension, the move is sequencing: build a ladder.

1. **Anchor in their current model.** Name the constraints they already recognize.
2. **Show one concrete “lightbulb.”** A demo, a tiny win, a before/after they can see.
3. **Explain the mechanism only after the evidence.** Don’t lead with the spaceship.
4. **Repeat until the new idea becomes *thinkable*.** Not “believable.” Thinkable.

This is why “cheat sheets” work. They don’t just explain. They narrow the gap enough for people to try the thing once and feel it.

## Terraforming the Comprehension Gap

This is one reason I care so much about “terraforming” work: changing the environment so the right move becomes normal.

In a team or org, comprehension is part of that environment. You can’t terraform what people can’t yet *see*.

So the sequencing matters:

- **Open the horizon:** make the lightbulb visible (small wins, concrete artifacts, guided first runs).
- **Then terraform:** make the path durable (defaults, guardrails, paved roads).

If you skip the first step and go straight to mandates, you get compliance theater at best and backlash at worst.

This ties directly to {% post_url 2026-01-26-open-then-terraform %}: terraforming is how you make gains stick, but first you have to get the gain into the collective solution space.

Another way to say it: the best version of agile was always “turn learning into shared structure.”
What we need now is a version that turns learning into *terrain*—guardrails, defaults, and practices that make harnessing agents feel normal instead of heroic.

## A Practical Script

When you feel that “I’m not being heard” moment, try aiming for the frame first:

- “What would make this true in your world?”
- “What constraint are you optimizing for here?”
- “If I could show a 5-minute demo that proves one part of this, which part should it be?”
- “What’s the smallest version of this that would be useful?”
- “What would you need to see to believe it’s not hand-wavy?”

The goal is not to win an argument. It’s to build shared representation.

Because if the gap is comprehension, the fix isn’t more force.
It’s a better ladder.
