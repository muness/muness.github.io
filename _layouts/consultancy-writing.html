Start with a hard question

We made one task cheaper. Why didn't the whole workflow improve?

Making an output cheap usually moves the constraint. The work may now need more evaluation, exception handling, judgment, or coordination. Trace the whole job before deciding what the new system made unnecessary—and what it made more important.

From the work: In one client system, a performance-review draft was assembled from existing evidence. The manager reviewed and revised the draft and owned the final review.

How does an agent stop making the same kind of mistake?

The correction has to survive the run. Preserve the failed case, the expected result, the accepted rule, and the evidence for the fix somewhere the next workflow can use and test. The model does not learn the business by itself; the surrounding system learns.

From the work: In a client pipeline, a failed case became an explicit check. The domain expert supplied the case and expected result; the workflow used it to separate an implementation repair from a proposed rule change.

How do we know whether we're building the right thing?

Name what someone should become able to do before choosing the feature. Then make the mechanism and feedback explicit: why should this change their behavior, and what would tell us that it did? The deliverable is evidence; the outcome is the change in the user's work.

From the work: For expert search, the target was not “ship semantic search.” It was to let the team inspect why a profile appeared, challenge the result, and change the ranking.
