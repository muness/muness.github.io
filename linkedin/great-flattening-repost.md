## Post

Companies add management layers for a fairly boring reason: coordination gets expensive.

As a company grows, someone has to gather status, settle priorities, translate decisions, resolve conflicts, and prevent every exception from reaching the top. A new layer is worthwhile when it removes more confusion and delay than it adds.

AI changes which management work pays for itself. Software can gather status, route routine decisions, condense information, and help each worker cover more ground. That reduces the value of management as information handling. At the same time, the resulting speed and volume increase the need for judgment, coaching, conflict resolution, local knowledge, exception handling, and accountability. The administrative work gets cheaper; the human work becomes the constraint.

That creates pressure to rebuild the organization, but it does not push every company in the same direction. Some will give teams more context and authority so decisions happen closer to the work. Others will keep authority at the top and use AI to monitor more people and issue more detailed instructions. Some are effectively rebuilding from scratch: gutting middle management before they have built a replacement, then being forced by burnout, rework, and executive overload to rediscover which coordination, coaching, and judgment were necessary complements to the faster production system.

Both stories are shallow: easy narratives that explain little. Coinbase says AI made a smaller workforce possible, but does not show which work disappeared, what happened to quality, or who picked up the review and maintenance. The article says flattening is an authoritarian power grab, but offers no evidence that this is the main cause.

Here is what I expect:

- The first cuts will hit managers who look like status collectors and decision routers, because software can copy that visible work. Their hidden work —carrying context, building trust, resolving conflict, and catching bad plans — will disappear too, making coordination worse.
- Companies will widen management spans too far: Visibility does not replace coaching or exception handling, so burnout and rework will force some of the layer back under new titles.
- Organizations will split. Some will give small teams context and authority. Others will encode priorities, permissions, metrics, and escalations into software, producing flatter org charts with tighter central control.

I do not know which structure will work. We have to try things, see what breaks, and learn. Calling people names will not help. We owe each other a plain explanation of our actions and decisions, and we need to pull together to fix what we get wrong.
