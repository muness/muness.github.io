# Aim: Strategy Has to Become Thinkable

**Aim:** Leaders will test whether people can predict, contest, and act on the mechanism behind a strategy before interpreting silence, repetition, or formal compliance as alignment.

**Why it matters:** A strategy can be stated clearly while different groups still operate from incompatible evidence, problem frames, mechanisms, and definitions of success. More communication does not create alignment when people cannot evaluate the same proposed move.

**Current State:** Leaders repeat a strategy, ask whether everyone understands, and interpret weak response as agreement, resistance, or an execution problem. Teams then optimize different local aims while using shared vocabulary.

**Desired State:** Readers build a shared, inspectable representation of the situation and use a short diagnostic sequence to locate whether coordination is failing at shared reality, problem framing, behavioral aim, governed action, or retained learning.

## Mechanism

**Change:** Publish a strategy essay that combines the comprehension-gap insight with a five-question operating sequence: What situation are we responding to? What problem are we solving? What behavior should change through what mechanism? What keeps action aligned and interruptible? What will be different next time?

**Hypothesis:** Treating useful disagreement and prediction as evidence of comprehension will help leaders distinguish a communication gap from a framing, strategy, execution, or learning failure and choose an intervention appropriate to the actual break.

**Assumptions:**

- A real episode can show a proposal moving from unevaluable to meaningfully contestable.
- The essay can define the relationship between this diagnostic and the author's `win-win-win` strategy framing precisely.
- Shared representations such as causal models, worked examples, executable scenarios, diagrams, or before-and-after traces can make a mechanism available for collective judgment.
- The five questions work better as a causal sequence than as five branded organizational dysfunctions.

**Misunderstanding Signal:** Readers reduce the essay to better presentation, persuasion technique, stakeholder buy-in, or another named alignment framework without changing how they test shared understanding.

## Feedback

**Signal:** Readers use the questions to identify a specific break in a live strategy conversation, produce more concrete objections or predictions, and change a framing or decision rather than merely restating the strategy more clearly.

**Timeframe:** Evaluate reader examples and reported use of the diagnostic during the first month after publication.

## Guardrails

- Anchor the essay in a real episode; do not substitute an invented organizational fable.
- Do not use the SQL simulator as proof of changed comprehension unless that behavior was observed.
- State the exact `win-win-win` relationship before drafting that section.
- Avoid claims about intelligence, resistance, or psychology that the evidence cannot support.
- Keep the five questions in plain actor-and-action language rather than Open Horizons command vocabulary.
- Treat disagreement as useful evidence, not as proof that the strategy is correct.
